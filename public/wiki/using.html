Wiki / Using FT8AF

From first launch
to a logged QSO.

1Install & first launch

Grab FT8AF from the GitHub releases page as a free APK, or from Google Play for $3.50 — the apps are identical. Full install instructions live on the Download page.

The first time you open FT8AF, Android will ask for two permissions:

  • USB device access — needed for CAT control and rig audio. Choose Always if you want the app to auto-reconnect when you replug the cable.
  • Microphone — needed if you ever want to decode from the phone's mic instead of a wired radio. You can deny this if you only run USB.

2Set your callsign & grid

Open the Settings tab and fill in the operator card at the top. These two fields drive everything the app transmits, so it's worth getting right before you key up.

Callsign
Your licensed callsign. Suffix portable indicators with a slash (e.g. K1AF/M) if you're operating mobile or portable.
Grid square
4- or 6-character Maidenhead locator (e.g. FN42 or FN42hk). FT8AF will offer to fill it from GPS if location is granted.
Audio freq
Your transmit audio frequency in Hz, inside the 3 kHz FT8 segment. 1500 is a sane starting default.
TX delay
Milliseconds the app waits after PTT before sending tones. 400 works for most CAT-controlled rigs.

3Connect your radio

FT8AF talks to your rig over a single USB-C connection — both CAT (frequency, PTT) and audio (RX, TX) ride on the same cable. Modern Android phones connect directly with USB-C; older devices need a USB-OTG adapter. Your phone must support USB host mode.

  1. Plug in. Connect your phone to the rig's USB port. Android will prompt for permission to talk to the device — accept it, and tick Always so the app can reconnect later.
  2. Pick your rig. In Settings, open the Rig picker and choose your model (IC-7300, FT-891, IC-705, etc.). FT8AF includes a fix that forces the FT-891 to 3000 Hz bandwidth in DATA-USB — you don't need to set that yourself.
  3. Set control mode to CAT. Under Control, choose CAT. (VOX, RTS, and DTR are available if your setup needs them, but CAT is what you want with a modern HF rig over USB.)
  4. Choose USB connection. Under Connection type, leave it on USB. Confirm CI-V address and baud rate match your rig — defaults are usually correct.
  5. Set frequency. Tap the band button on the top bar to switch between 160m–6m FT8 segments, or punch in a custom frequency from Settings.

No rig? You can still explore.

If you grant the microphone permission, FT8AF will decode audio from your phone's mic — hold it up to a speaker hooked to any FT8-active radio and you'll see the same decode list. Useful for learning the UI before you wire up CAT.

4Decode the band

The Decode tab is where you'll spend most of your operating time. Every 15 seconds, at the FT8 period boundary, the app processes the last slot of audio and drops the new decodes into the list.

What each column tells you:

S / dB
Signal-to-noise ratio. Values from roughly −24 (barely decodable) to +15 (very strong).
Δt
Time offset in seconds. Decodes outside ±2.5 s indicate the other op's clock is off — or yours is.
Freq
Audio frequency in Hz inside the 3 kHz segment. Tap to make this your TX audio frequency.
Message
The actual FT8 payload — CQ calls, signal reports, RR73, etc. Country flags and your callsign are highlighted.
Distance
Great-circle distance from your grid to the decoded station, when their grid is known.

5Read the waterfall

The Spectrum / Waterfall tab shows the full 3 kHz FT8 passband over time. Newer audio is at the top, older at the bottom — a horizontal red line marks each FT8 period boundary so you can read timing at a glance.

Tap-to-tune. Tap any visible signal in the waterfall to set your TX audio frequency to that slot. The transmit marker stays locked to your actual TX audio thanks to the alignment fix in FT8AF.

6Call a station

You have two ways into a QSO:

  1. Answer a CQ. Tap a CQ row in the decode list, or tap that station's signal on the waterfall. FT8AF queues a response and waits for the next TX slot.
  2. Call CQ yourself. Hit the CQ button on the TX strip at the bottom of the screen. Use the TX1 / TX2 toggle to pick which 15-second slot you want to transmit on — the empty one, generally.

Watch the Active QSO monitor panel above the TX strip. It tracks who you're working and what step of the auto-sequence you're at. If multiple stations come back to your CQ, the caller queue keeps the others lined up — you finish the current QSO instead of bouncing to whoever's loudest.

7Run the auto-sequence

FT8AF handles the standard 6-message FT8 exchange automatically once you've selected a target. You'll see the panel step through:

  1. Call. EA8DH K1AF FN42
  2. Send report. EA8DH K1AF −09
  3. Roger report. K1AF EA8DH R−06
  4. Confirm. EA8DH K1AF RR73
  5. Acknowledge & close. K1AF EA8DH 73
  6. Log. The QSO drops into the logbook and uploads (if enabled).

If you need to bail mid-QSO, tap Stop on the TX strip — the app drops the queue and goes back to listening. The TX1 / TX2 slot toggle lets you flip transmit periods without losing your target.

8Logging & uploads

Every completed QSO is written to the on-device QSO Logs tab. If you've configured upload services, it also flies up to them the moment the contact closes — no ADIF export, no copying calls by hand at the end of the night.

Cloudlog

Open Settings → Cloudlog, paste your Cloudlog URL and API key, and toggle the service on. New QSOs upload automatically. If a contact fails to upload, it stays in the logbook with a retry indicator.

QRZ

Open Settings → QRZ, sign in with your QRZ Logbook API key, and toggle uploads on. Same automatic behavior as Cloudlog.

You can run both at once — most operators do, with Cloudlog as their primary log and QRZ for visibility.

9Map & statistics

The Map tab plots every decoded station on a world map, with a heading line drawn from your grid. CQ callers get a megaphone icon so you can spot openings visually instead of scanning the list.

The QSO Logs tab also includes statistics — band breakdown, ITU zones worked, DXCC completion — so you can see at a glance which entities you still need.

10Field tips

  • Set your clock from the network before each session. FT8 demands accurate timing — Android's automatic time is usually fine; if you're somewhere without signal, sync from GPS or NTP.
  • Use the hardware volume buttons to set TX drive level mid-QSO. FT8AF maps them to TX volume so you don't have to fiddle with the on-screen slider while transmitting.
  • Watch the QSO monitor when conditions are crowded. The caller queue prevents the classic "two stations both think you came back to them" mess.
  • Star the GitHub repo if you want a nudge when a new release lands. Or grab the Play Store build and updates happen by themselves.
